How Temperature Change Affects Austrlian Foret Fires

Forest fires are a major issue in Australia that causes heavy damage to the wildlife as well as sometimes impacting the livelihoods of Australian citizens. This is an issue that is ongoing and will continue to impact Australia until we are able to find ways to prevent it completely.

From this map we are able to see that the states in Australia that suffer the most forest fires are the northern states which have a more tropical climate. The warmer climate causes the forests to be dryer and much easier to burn hence being at a much higher risk of forest fires starting.

Forest fires are also very dangerous and have the ability to cause a lot of damage. From the cumulative fire damage chart seen below the damage that forest fires cause on a yearly basis is millions of hectares of forest. Interesting data points to point out is the massive spike in damages of forest fires in NSW in 2020 where massive bushfires tore through the state, burning through 4.8 million hectares of forest. This massive spike in damages is also reflected by the number of planned fires within the state, only about 6% of fires that year being unplanned. The lack of preparedness and spike in temperature the year prior as well as many other factors has led to the devastating bushfires that year.

There is an ongoing trend that temperatures are rising in Australia. The average temperature has had a steady increase since the 1980s and the trend has only gone up. This means much warmer temperatures being more common which will only increase the risk of bushfires occurring.

Whilst we tend to associate forest fires as a natural disaster from the pie chart we are able to see that in the majority of the states actually conduct planned fires. Generally however we are also able to see that only about 50% of forest fires in all Australian states from 2016-2021 are unplanned natural disasters.

From the limited overlapping time of Australian temperature anomaly data and the forest fires data we see that there is limited correlation. There seems to be a lagged trend where incidents of fires occur more frequently the year after a much warmer year. Since how forest fires start is more than just the climate we can't draw any conclusions however from this comparison, but it does give us an understanding of how temperature is able to affect the rates of which forest fires occur.
